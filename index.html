Case Study — Enterprise Information Architecture

Information Architecture for a 200-person Global Team

Designing a unified Confluence space across 12 countries — where the hardest decisions weren't about layout, but about permissions, governance, and how people would actually maintain it.

Client

Mizuho Bank × Atlassian

Role

Sole Designer & IA Lead

Scale

200+ users, 12 countries

Context

Accenture Japan, 2024

01 — Context

The problem wasn't missing content.
It was missing structure.

Mizuho Bank's Atlassian implementation involved 200+ employees across 12 countries. Project documentation existed — but it was scattered. There was no unified entry point, no clear ownership, and finding the right file meant knowing who to ask.

The brief was to design a Confluence space that would actually work at scale: discoverable for anyone, maintainable without central intervention, and legible across teams who had never worked together in the same system before.

01

Location first, not function

The first structural question: what goes at the top level — geography (12 locations) or content type (departments, functions)?

I chose location as the primary axis. The reason was technical as much as organisational. Confluence's permission system follows the page hierarchy. A functional top level — "QA", "Finance", "Operations" — would require permissions to span across locations, making them complex to set up and impossible to delegate cleanly.

Putting location first meant each of the 12 locations could own its own space, with permissions handed to a local lead. Simple to administer, legible to everyone involved.

Permission model drove hierarchy, not content taxonomy
02

One template, replicated twelve times

Rather than designing each location's space individually, I designed one master structure and replicated it across all 12 locations. Each followed the same pattern: 6 departments, each with 2–4 function-specific pages beneath.

This made the system scalable — adding a new location meant copying a known structure, not designing from scratch. It also made navigation predictable: once you understood one location's space, you understood all of them.

Scalable pattern over bespoke design
03

Distributed governance, not central control

Permissions for each location's space were assigned to the local lead, not managed centrally. This was a deliberate governance decision: a centrally managed system of this size would become a bottleneck — one person responsible for 12 locations worth of updates, access requests, and page changes.

Local ownership meant local accountability. In practice, it meant the system could stay current without depending on a single administrator.

Designed for sustainability, not just launch

04 — Stakeholder alignment

Designed alone,
validated together.

The initial structure was my decision. I then reviewed it with the team leader and the client, adjusting based on their feedback. The three-tier hierarchy held; adjustments were made at the department and page level to reflect how Mizuho's internal teams actually organised their work.

The alignment process also surfaced an additional requirement: PMO needed a single view across all project documentation — a top-level entry point that sat above the location structure. This became a dedicated PMO layer, giving leadership oversight without disrupting the location-based navigation for everyone else.
